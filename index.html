I. Heat Transfer (Conduction & BBR)

1. Thermal Conduction and Circuit Analogy

Conduction is the transfer of energy through particle collisions in contact, without particle migration [1]. The rate of heat flow ($P$) is analogous to electric current ($I = \Delta V / R$) [2].

Conduction Formulas: $$P = \frac{\Delta T}{R_{th}} \quad \text{(Unit: W)}$$ $$R_{th} = \frac{l}{k A} \quad \text{(Unit: K/W or } \degree\text{C/W)}$$

Where $l$ is thickness, $A$ is area, and $k$ is thermal conductivity [3, 4].

Thermal Circuit Rules

  • Series Combination: Layers are sequential (e.g., Brick then Insulation) [5]. $$\mathbf{R_{eq, series} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \ldots}$$

    The power ($P$) remains constant through each component in series [5, 6].

  • Parallel Combination: Heat flows through paths side-by-side (e.g., wooden studs alongside insulation) [7]. $$\mathbf{\frac{1}{R_{eq, parallel}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \ldots}$$

    The path with lower resistance (higher thermal conductivity $k$) carries more heat. Wooden studs form a thermal bridge because $k$ for wood is much higher than for typical insulation [8, 9].

  • Feeling Cold: An object feels cold if its temperature is lower than your hand *and* it has **high thermal conductivity** ($k$), allowing it to rapidly draw heat away [10].

2. Blackbody Radiation (BBR)

BBR describes EM radiation emitted based on temperature [11]. An ideal blackbody absorbs all incident radiation ($\epsilon=1$) [12].

BBR Laws: $$\mathbf{I_{emitted} = \epsilon \sigma T^4} \quad \text{(Stefan-Boltzmann Law)}$$ $$\mathbf{\lambda_{max} T = b} \quad \text{(Wien’s Displacement Law)}$$

If $T$ increases, $\lambda_{max}$ decreases (shifts to blue/UV), and total intensity ($I \propto T^4$) increases drastically [11, 13, 14]. **Bluish stars are hotter than reddish stars** [15, 16]. The theory originated from Planck’s proposal that energy is quantized: $E_{oscillation} = n h f$ [17].

Planetary Temperature Models

Equilibrium is reached when absorbed power equals radiated power [18].

No Atmosphere Model ($T_{eq}$): $$I_{absorbed} = I_{radiated} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{\sigma T_{eq}^4 = \frac{I_{rec}}{4} (1-a)}$$

Where $a$ is albedo (fraction reflected) [18, 19]. $I_{rec}$ is the intensity incident on the planet, $I_{rec} = P_{star} / (4 \pi r^2)$ [20, 21].

Greenhouse Effect (Atmosphere Present): $$\mathbf{T_s = T_{eq} \sqrt[22]{\frac{2}{2-\epsilon_{atm}}}} \quad \text{(Single Layer Model)}$$ $$\mathbf{T_s = T_{eq} \sqrt[22]{\frac{1}{1-\ln(1-\epsilon_{atm})}}} \quad \text{(Infinite Layer Model)}$$

Increasing the equilibrium temperature ($T_{eq}$) has the largest impact on surface temperature ($T_s$) in the single-layer model [9, 23]. The infinite layer model predicts **divergence** ($T_s \to \infty$) as atmospheric emissivity ($\epsilon \to 1$), contradicting the Second Law of Thermodynamics [24, 25].

Radiative Forcing ($\Delta F$): $$\mathbf{\Delta F_{CO_2} = 5.35 \frac{W}{m^2} \ln\left(\frac{C}{278 \text{ ppm}}\right)}$$ $$\mathbf{\Delta T \approx 0.55 \frac{\degree\text{C} \cdot m^2}{W} \cdot \Delta F}$$

II. Wave Phenomena and Optics

1. Wave Fundamentals and Parameters

General Wave Parameters: $$D(x, t) = A \cos(\omega t \mp kx + \phi_0)$$ $$\omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} \quad \text{and} \quad v = \lambda f = \frac{\omega}{k}$$

For a standing wave on a string fixed at both ends, frequencies are quantized: $f_n = n (v/2L)$, $n=1, 2, 3, \ldots$ [26, 27].

Intensity: $$\mathbf{I \propto A^2}$$ $$\mathbf{I_{sound} = \frac{(\Delta P_{max})^2}{2\rho v}}$$

2. Double Slit Interference (Young's Experiment)

Interference is the superposition of coherent waves [28]. It provides experimental proof that light is a wave [29].

Phase and Path Difference: $$\Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \Delta r + \Delta \phi_0$$ $$\Delta r \approx d \sin(\theta) \quad \text{(Parallel Ray Approx)}$$
Maxima (Constructive, Bright Fringes): $$\mathbf{d \sin(\theta) = m \lambda} \quad (m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots)$$ $$\mathbf{y_{max} \approx \frac{m \lambda L}{d}} \quad \text{(Small Angle Approx, } L \gg y)$$
Minima (Destructive, Dark Fringes): $$\mathbf{d \sin(\theta) = (m + 1/2) \lambda} \quad (m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots)$$ $$\mathbf{y_{min} \approx \frac{(m + 1/2) \lambda L}{d}} \quad \text{(Small Angle Approx)}$$

The minimum slit separation ($d$) required to observe at least one interference minimum is $\mathbf{d = \lambda/2}$ [30].

Intensity and Phasors

For two waves of equal amplitude ($A$):

$$A_{tot} = 2A \cos\left(\frac{\Delta\phi}{2}\right)$$ $$\mathbf{I_{tot} = I_{max} \cos^2\left(\frac{\Delta \phi}{2}\right)}$$

Phasors: Represent oscillating motion as vectors (amplitude = length, phase = angle). Resultant amplitude is the vector sum [31, 32]. For $N$ slits, minima occur when phasors form a closed polygon (vector sum is zero), such as $\Delta \phi = 2\pi/N$ [33].

3. Thin Film Interference

Involves interference between waves reflected at the top ($n_1 \to n_{film}$) and bottom ($n_{film} \to n_3$) interfaces [34].

  • Phase Shift on Reflection: A **$\pi$ radian ($1/2 \lambda$) phase shift** occurs only when light reflects off a boundary leading to a medium with a **higher** index of refraction ($n_{incident} < n_{reflected}$) [26].
  • Conditions: Interference depends on $2t$ (path length in the film) relative to the internal wavelength ($\lambda_n = \lambda/n_{film}$), plus the phase shifts from reflection [26].

4. Diffraction and Resolution

Diffraction is the bending or spreading of waves around edges [29, 35].

Single Slit Diffraction

The pattern results from the interference of virtual wavelets across the single slit aperture [36, 37].

Minima (Dark Fringes) Location: $$\mathbf{a \sin(\theta) = m \lambda} \quad (m=\pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots \text{ only})$$

The central maximum is approximately **twice as wide** as the side maxima [37, 38]. If $\mathbf{\lambda/a > 1}$, no diffraction minima are observed [39].

Intensity: $$I = I_0 \left(\frac{\sin(\beta/2)}{\beta/2}\right)^2 \quad \text{where } \beta = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} a \sin(\theta)$$

Suppressed Maxima: In double-slit experiments, an interference bright fringe is suppressed if it coincides with a diffraction minimum ($d/a = m_{int}/m_{diff}$) [40, 41].

Diffraction Gratings

Used for spectroscopy; separates light into colors because angle depends on wavelength [42].

Maxima Location: $$\mathbf{d \sin(\theta) = m \lambda} \quad (m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots)$$

The $m=0$ order is **not dispersed** [42]. Higher orders overlap, e.g., violet end of $m=3$ overlaps the red end of $m=2$ [43].

Resolution (Rayleigh Criterion)

Defines the minimum angular separation ($\theta_{min}$) required to distinguish two point sources [44].

Minimum Angular Resolution: $$\mathbf{\theta_{min} \approx 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}} \quad \text{(Circular Aperture, diameter } D)$$ $$\mathbf{\theta_{min} \approx 1 \frac{\lambda}{a}} \quad \text{(Rectangular Aperture, width } a)$$

Resolution is improved by using a **larger aperture ($D$)** or a **shorter wavelength ($\lambda$)** (e.g., blue light resolves better than red light) [44, 45].

III. Modern Physics (Quantum Optics & Matter Waves)

1. Photoelectric Effect (Light as a Particle)

Einstein explained this effect by proposing the photon, confirming light's particle nature [46].

Photon Energy and Momentum: $$E_{photon} = h f = \frac{h c}{\lambda}$$ $$p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$
Photoelectric Equation: $$\mathbf{K_{max} = h f - \phi}$$ $$\mathbf{K_{max} = e |\Delta V_{stop}|}$$
  • Work Function ($\phi$): Minimum energy to eject an electron [47]. Related to the cutoff frequency ($f_c$): $\mathbf{\phi = h f_c}$ [20].
  • Kinetic Energy ($K_{max}$): Depends only on the **frequency ($f$)** of the light, not the intensity ($I$) [47, 48].
  • Intensity ($I$): Determines the **number of photons** ($N/\text{m}^2\text{s}$) and thus the electric current, but does not affect $K_{max}$ [48, 49].
  • Graphical Analysis: Plotting $K_{max}$ vs. $f$ yields a straight line with a **slope equal to $h$** (Planck's constant), regardless of the metal [47, 50].

2. Matter Waves and Quantization

De Broglie Hypothesis

Matter particles exhibit wave characteristics [51].

$$\mathbf{\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{\gamma m v}}$$

Confined Particles (Particle in a Box)

Confinement restricts the particle's wave function to standing waves, leading to quantized energy [52, 53].

Allowed Wavelengths (1D Box): $$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n} \quad (n=1, 2, 3, \ldots)$$
Allowed Energies: $$E_n = \frac{h^2 n^2}{8 m L^2}$$

Probability: The square of the wave function, $|\psi|^2$, represents the probability density of finding the particle. Maxima occur at the antinodes of the standing wave [54-56].

Bohr Model (Hydrogen Atom)

Applies quantization to the electron's angular momentum to ensure stable orbits [57].

Quantized Angular Momentum (Bohr Postulate): $$\mathbf{L = m v r = n \hbar}$$
Energy Levels: $$\mathbf{E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}}$$
Photon Energy for Transitions: $$E_{photon} = |E_i - E_f| = \frac{h c}{\lambda}$$

Electrons absorb a photon with the **exact energy** corresponding to $E_f - E_i$ to jump up [58, 59]. The **Balmer series** consists of transitions dropping to the $n=2$ orbital (visible light) [60, 61]. Ionization occurs when an electron reaches $E_{\infty}=0$ [58].

IV. Special Relativity

1. Einstein's Postulates

  1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial (non-accelerating) frames [62].
  2. The speed of light ($c$) in vacuum is constant for all inertial observers [62].
Lorentz Factor: $$\mathbf{\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}}$$

2. Kinematic Effects

The definitions of proper time ($\Delta t_p$) and proper length ($L_p$) are critical [63].

  • Proper Time ($\Delta t_p$): The shortest time interval, measured in the frame where the two events occur at the **same spatial position** [63-65].
    Time Dilation: $$\mathbf{\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_p}$$
  • Proper Length ($L_p$): The longest measured length, measured in the frame where the object is **at rest** [63, 64].
    Length Contraction: $$\mathbf{L = \frac{L_p}{\gamma}}$$
  • Simultaneity: Events simultaneous in one frame are generally **not simultaneous** in a moving frame [66, 67].

3. Relativistic Energy and Momentum

Relativistic Energy and Momentum: $$\mathbf{p = \gamma m v}$$ $$\mathbf{E = \gamma m c^2}$$ $$E_{0} = m c^2 \quad \text{(Rest Energy)}$$ $$\mathbf{K = (\gamma - 1) m c^2} \quad \text{(Kinetic Energy)}$$
Energy-Momentum Relation: $$\mathbf{E^2 = p^2 c^2 + (m c^2)^2}$$

For a particle with zero mass (like a photon), the relation simplifies to $E=pc$ [68]. If total energy $E$ equals $N$ times the rest energy $E_0$, then $\gamma = N$ [69].
