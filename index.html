I. Heat Transfer (Conduction)

Temperature (T) is related to kinetic energy (K). Heat transfer mechanisms include convection, conduction, and radiation. Conduction involves energy transfer through contact and particle collision, without particle migration.

Thermal Conduction Formulas

The rate of heat transfer (Power, $P$) due to conduction is analogous to electric current ($I = \Delta V / R$).

Rate of Heat Transfer (Power): $$P = \frac{\Delta T}{R_{th}}$$ (Unit: W)
Thermal Resistance: $$R_{th} = \frac{l}{k A}$$ (Unit: °C/W or K/W)
(where $l$ is length/thickness, $k$ is thermal conductivity, $A$ is cross-sectional area, $\Delta T$ is temperature difference).

Thermal conductivity ($k$) is a material property (e.g., air $k \approx 0.026 \text{ W/m°C}$, aluminum $k \approx 237 \text{ W/m°C}$, stainless steel $k \approx 15 \text{ W/m°C}$). Materials with high $k$ (like metal) feel cold if they are at a lower temperature than your hand because they quickly draw heat away.

Thermal Circuit Analogy (Series and Parallel)

Walls and materials resisting heat flow can be modeled as resistors.

  • Series Combination: Heat flows sequentially through layers (e.g., brick, insulation, drywall). The power $P$ is constant through all components.
    Equivalent Resistance (Series): $$R_{eq, series} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ...$$
  • Parallel Combination: Heat flows simultaneously through different paths (e.g., wooden studs alongside insulation). The total power is the sum of powers through each path ($P_{tot} = P_1 + P_2 + ...$). The path with lower resistance (higher $k$) carries more heat (a "thermal bridge").
    Equivalent Resistance (Parallel): $$\frac{1}{R_{eq, parallel}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + ...$$

Example: Series Resistance (Problem 2/3 Concept)

A composite wall has resistance layers summed up: $R_{total} = R_{Brick} + R_{Insulation} + R_{Drywall}$. If $\Delta T = 35^\circ \text{C}$ and $R_{total} = 0.1090 \text{ K/W}$, the heat loss rate is $P = \Delta T / R_{total} \approx 321 \text{ W}$. Cost analysis involves multiplying the power loss by time and energy cost ($10 \text{c/kWh}$ for 4 months of winter heating).

II. Blackbody Radiation & Planetary Temperature

Blackbody Characteristics

  • An ideal blackbody absorbs all incident radiation ($\epsilon=1$) and emits radiation based purely on its temperature. Stars and tungsten filaments are good approximations ($\epsilon \approx 1$).
  • Emission depends on temperature ($T$). Color depends on temperature (hotter objects glow blueish, cooler ones reddish).
  • Energy is quantized, originating from Planck's proposal based on blackbody radiation ($E_{oscillation} = n h f$).

Blackbody Formulas (BBR)

Stefan-Boltzmann Law (Intensity emitted): $$I_{emitted} = \epsilon \sigma T^4$$ (Unit: W/m²)
Total Power Emitted (Luminosity): $$P_{emitted} = \epsilon A \sigma T^4$$
Wien’s Displacement Law (Peak Wavelength): $$\lambda_{max} T = b$$ (where $b = 2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m} \cdot \text{K}$)
Conceptual Relation: If T increases, $\lambda_{max}$ decreases (shifts to blue/UV). Total intensity ($I \propto T^4$) increases drastically.

Example: Wien's Law and Temperature (Problem 9/7 Concept)

If the temperature of a blackbody is tripled (3T): $\lambda_{max}$ becomes $1/3$ of the original $\lambda_{max}$. The total emitted intensity ($I \propto T^4$) increases by a factor of $3^4 = 81$. Bluish stars are hotter than reddish stars.

Planetary Temperature Modeling

Planets achieve equilibrium when absorbed power equals radiated power ($P_{absorbed} = P_{radiated}$).

Intensity received from a star at distance $r$: $$I_{rec} = \frac{P_{star}}{4 \pi r^2}$$
Equilibrium Temperature ($T_{eq}$) (No Atmosphere Model): $$\sigma T_{eq}^4 = \frac{I_{rec}}{4} (1-a)$$ (where $a$ is albedo, the fraction reflected)

Greenhouse Effect Models (Atmosphere present)

These models use the planet's equilibrium temperature ($T_{eq}$, calculated ignoring atmosphere) and atmospheric emissivity ($\epsilon$) to find the surface temperature ($T_s$).

  • Single Layer Model: The atmosphere absorbs IR radiation emitted by the surface ($\epsilon \approx 0.71$ for Earth).
    $$T_s = T_{eq} \sqrt{\frac{2}{2-\epsilon}}$$
    In this model, if $\epsilon=1$ (perfect trap), $T_s = T_{eq} \sqrt{2} \approx 1.19 T_{eq}$.
  • Infinite Layer Model: Accounts for multiple atmospheric layers (more realistic, often used for $\epsilon$ near 1).
    $$T_s = T_{eq} \sqrt{\frac{1}{1-\ln(1-\epsilon)}}$$
    This expression predicts that if $\epsilon \to 1$, the surface temperature $T_s$ diverges (approaches infinity), which is physically impossible and contradicts the Second Law of Thermodynamics.
  • Radiative Forcing ($\Delta F$): Measures the change in the net intensity absorbed by the surface due to human impact, often related to CO2 concentration ($C$).
    $$\Delta F_{CO_2} = 5.35 \frac{W}{m^2} \ln\left(\frac{C}{278 \text{ ppm}}\right)$$ $$\Delta T \approx 0.55 \frac{^\circ \text{C} \cdot m^2}{W} \cdot \Delta F$$

III. Wave Phenomena and Optics

Wave Fundamentals

Waves are described by displacement $D(x, t) = A \cos(\omega t \mp kx + \phi_0)$. Key relations include:

Angular Frequency, Frequency, Period: $$\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi/T$$ Wave Speed: $$v = \lambda f = \omega/k$$
Intensity ($I$) is proportional to amplitude squared ($A^2$ or $E^2$).

A. Interference (Double Slit)

Interference occurs from the superposition of waves ($\mathbf{D_{net} = \sum D_i}$). It requires coherent light ($\Delta \phi_0 = 0$).

Phase and Path Difference

The total phase difference ($\Delta \phi$) depends on the path length difference ($\Delta r = r_2 - r_1$) and the initial phase difference ($\Delta \phi_0$).

Phase Difference: $$\Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \Delta r + \Delta \phi_0$$
Path Difference (Double Slit, parallel ray approx): $$\Delta r \approx d \sin(\theta)$$

Maxima (Constructive Interference)

Occurs when waves arrive in phase, $\Delta \phi$ is a multiple of $2\pi$.

Angular Position: $$d \sin(\theta) = m \lambda$$ (where $m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, ...$)
Position on Screen (Small Angle Approx, $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta \approx y/L$): $$y_{max} \approx \frac{m \lambda L}{d}$$

Minima (Destructive Interference)

Occurs when waves arrive $180^\circ$ out of phase, $\Delta \phi$ is an odd multiple of $\pi$.

Angular Position: $$d \sin(\theta) = (m + 1/2) \lambda$$ (where $m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, ...$)
Position on Screen (Small Angle Approx): $$y_{min} \approx \frac{(m + 1/2) \lambda L}{d}$$

The smallest slit separation ($d$) required to see at least one interference minimum is $d = \lambda/2$.

Intensity in Double Slit Interference

If two waves have the same amplitude ($A$), the total amplitude $A_{tot} = 2A \cos(\Delta\phi/2)$.

Total Intensity: $$I_{tot} = I_{max} \cos^2\left(\frac{\Delta \phi}{2}\right)$$

Phasor diagrams can be used to graphically represent the superposition of waves; the resultant amplitude is the vector sum of the phasors.

B. Thin Film Interference

Interference occurs between light waves reflected at the top and bottom boundaries of a thin layer.

Phase Shifts Due to Reflection

A $\pi$ radian (or $1/2 \lambda$) phase shift occurs upon reflection if the light moves from a medium with a lower index of refraction ($n_1$) to a medium with a higher index of refraction ($n_2$) ($n_1 < n_2$). No phase shift occurs if $n_1 > n_2$.

Conditions for Maxima/Minima (In terms of thickness $t$)

The path difference is approximately $2t$ (for normal incidence), and the effective wavelength inside the film is $\lambda_n = \lambda / n_{film}$. The conditions must account for any phase shifts incurred at the two interfaces.

For Reflection (where $m=0, 1, 2, ...$):
Case 1: Total phase shift (reflection + path) = $2m\pi$ (Constructive Interference $\Rightarrow$ Bright Reflection)
Case 2: Total phase shift (reflection + path) = $(2m+1)\pi$ (Destructive Interference $\Rightarrow$ Dark Reflection / Strong Transmission)
*Note: The specific equations for $2t$ depend on the number of $\pi$ phase shifts occurring at the two interfaces.*

C. Diffraction and Resolution

Diffraction is the ability of waves to bend or spread around obstacles. Interference is inherent in the resulting pattern.

Single Slit Diffraction

The diffraction pattern consists of a bright central maximum flanked by much fainter, narrower secondary maxima.

Minima (Dark Fringes) Location: $$a \sin(\theta) = m \lambda$$ (where $a$ is slit width, $m=\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, ...$, but $m \neq 0$)
Intensity Distribution: $$I = I_0 \left(\frac{\sin(\beta/2)}{\beta/2}\right)^2$$ where $\beta = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} a \sin(\theta)$

The width of the central maximum is approximately twice the width of the secondary bright maxima. If $\lambda/a > 1$, no diffraction minima are observed, and the central max fills the entire screen.

Diffraction Gratings

A diffraction grating has a large number of closely spaced parallel slits. It is used for spectroscopy (separating light into color components).

Maxima Location (Bright, narrow bands): $$d \sin(\theta) = m \lambda$$ (where $d$ is the spacing between grooves/slits, $m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, ...$)

The $m=0$ order is not spread into color components. Higher orders ($m \ge 1$) show greater angular spread but can overlap (e.g., violet end of $m=3$ overlaps red end of $m=2$).

If UV light ($\lambda=350 \text{ nm}$) forms maxima, red light ($\lambda=700 \text{ nm}$) will form maxima at the same angular positions if the grating slit spacing is doubled to $2d$.

Resolution (Rayleigh Criterion)

Resolution is the ability to distinguish two separate sources, limited by diffraction.

Minimum Angular Resolution ($\theta_{min}$):
Rectangular Aperture (Slit width $a$): $$\theta_{min} \approx 1 \frac{\lambda}{a}$$
Circular Aperture (Diameter $D$): $$\theta_{min} \approx 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}$$

The smaller $\theta_{min}$ is, the better the resolving power. To improve resolution, one should use a larger aperture ($D$) or a shorter wavelength ($\lambda$) (e.g., blue light resolves better than red light).

IV. Modern Physics: Quantum Optics & Matter Waves

The dual nature of light and matter confirms that energy is quantized.

A. The Photoelectric Effect (Light as a Particle)

The photoelectric effect is the ejection of electrons (photoelectrons) from a metal surface when illuminated by light. This phenomenon is explained by Einstein using the concept of the photon (a packet of energy).

Photon Energy and Momentum

Photon Energy: $$E_{photon} = h f = \frac{h c}{\lambda}$$
Photon Momentum (for light, $m=0$): $$p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$

Key Observations and Explanation (Classical Failure)

The Photoelectric Equation links the discrete energy of a photon ($h f$) to the kinetic energy of the emitted electron ($K_{max}$) and the energy barrier ($\phi$).

Photoelectric Equation: $$\mathbf{K_{max} = h f - \phi}$$
Where $K_{max} = \frac{1}{2} m v_{max}^2 = e |\Delta V_{stop}|$
  • Maximum Kinetic Energy ($K_{max}$): Depends only on frequency ($f$), not intensity ($I$). Increasing $f$ increases $K_{max}$.
  • Cutoff Frequency ($f_c$): If $f < f_c$, no electrons are ejected, regardless of intensity. At the cutoff, $K_{max}=0$, so $\phi = h f_c = h c / \lambda_c$.
  • Intensity ($I$): Intensity determines the *quantity* (number) of photons per second, which, in turn, dictates the measured current (number of ejected electrons). Increasing $I$ increases the current, but does not affect $K_{max}$.
  • Stopping Potential ($\Delta V_{stop}$): The voltage required to reduce the photocurrent to zero; related to $K_{max}$ by $K_{max} = e |\Delta V_{stop}|$.

Graphical Analysis of Photoelectric Effect (P01/P09 Concept)

A plot of $K_{max}$ versus frequency ($f$) yields a straight line: $K_{max} = h f - \phi$. The slope of this line is $\mathbf{h}$ (Planck's constant). The horizontal intercept ($K_{max}=0$) gives the cutoff frequency ($f_c$). The vertical intercept ($f=0$) gives the negative work function ($-\phi$).

B. Matter Waves (Matter as a Wave)

Particles of matter (like electrons or protons) also exhibit wave characteristics, summarized by the De Broglie hypothesis.

De Broglie Wavelength: $$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{\gamma m v}$$ (Use $p=mv$ for non-relativistic speed)

For macroscopic objects, the wavelength is extremely small, making the wave nature unmeasurable. When elementary particles pass through double slits, they produce interference patterns, governed by the same rules as light interference ($d \sin\theta = m \lambda$), using the De Broglie wavelength.

C. Confined Particles and Atomic Structure (Bohr Model)

Confined Particles (Particle in a Box)

When a particle is confined (e.g., electron in a box), its wave function must form a standing wave ($\lambda_n = 2L/n$). This results in quantized kinetic energy levels.

Allowed Wavelengths (1D Box): $$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n}$$ (where $n=1, 2, 3, ...$)
Allowed Kinetic Energies: $$E_n = \frac{h^2 n^2}{8 m L^2}$$

Bohr Model (Hydrogen Atom)

The Bohr model proposed that electrons reside in stable orbits only if their angular momentum is quantized ($L = n \hbar$) to prevent the electron from spiraling into the nucleus (as predicted by classical E&M).

Quantized Angular Momentum: $$L = m v r = n \frac{h}{2\pi} = n \hbar$$
Allowed Orbital Radii: $$r_n = n^2 a_0$$ (where $a_0 = 0.0529 \text{ nm}$ is the Bohr radius)
Energy Levels (Total Energy $K+U$): $$E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}$$ (where $n=1$ is the ground state)

Electrons absorb energy (e.g., collision, or photon absorption) to jump to higher levels ($n_{low} \to n_{high}$). Electrons emit a photon when dropping to a lower level ($n_{high} \to n_{low}$).

Emitted/Absorbed Photon Energy: $$E_{photon} = |E_i - E_f|$$

Specific transitions correspond to spectral series (e.g., Balmer series transitions drop to $n=2$, corresponding to visible light). Ionization requires enough energy to move the electron from level $E_n$ to $E_\infty = 0$.

Wave Function Interpretation

The square of the wave function, $|\psi|^2$, represents the probability density of finding the particle at a specific location, analogous to intensity ($I$) in wave physics. Antinodes in a confined particle correspond to maximum probability.

V. Special Relativity

Einstein's Two Postulates

  1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial (non-accelerating) frames of reference.
  2. The speed of light in vacuum ($c$) is the same for all inertial observers, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer.

Lorentz Factor ($\gamma$)

Relativistic effects are governed by the Lorentz factor ($\gamma$), where $v$ is the relative speed between frames.

$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \beta^2}}$$
(where $\beta = v/c$). Note that $\gamma \geq 1$.

Time Dilation and Length Contraction

The time interval and length measured depend on the observer's frame of reference. The concepts of "proper time" ($\Delta t_p$) and "proper length" ($L_p$) are crucial definitions.

  • Proper Time ($\Delta t_p$): The shortest time interval measured between two events that occur at the same position in the observer's frame (the rest frame of the object/clock).
    Time Dilation: $$\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_p$$ (Observer $\Delta t$ is longer)
  • Proper Length ($L_p$): The longest length measured between two points/objects in the frame where the object is at rest.
    Length Contraction: $$L = \frac{L_p}{\gamma} = L_p \sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$$ (Observer $L$ is shorter)
  • Relativity of Simultaneity: Two events judged to be simultaneous in one frame are generally NOT simultaneous in another moving frame.

Relativistic Energy and Momentum

Linear momentum and energy must be redefined to be conserved across all inertial frames.

Relativistic Momentum: $$\mathbf{p = \gamma m v}$$
Total Energy: $$\mathbf{E = \gamma m c^2} = K + m c^2$$
Rest Energy (Energy stored in mass): $$E_0 = m c^2$$
Relativistic Kinetic Energy: $$\mathbf{K = (\gamma - 1) m c^2}$$
Energy-Momentum Relation (useful for derivations): $$\mathbf{E^2 = p^2 c^2 + (m c^2)^2}$$

Example: Total Energy and Speed (Problem 10 Concept)

If a particle's total energy ($E$) is three times its rest energy ($E_0=m c^2$), then $E = 3 m c^2$. Since $E = \gamma m c^2$, we have $\gamma = 3$. This means $1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2} = 3$. Solving for $v$ yields $v = c \sqrt{8/9} \approx 0.94 c$.
