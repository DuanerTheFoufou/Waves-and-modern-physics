I. Heat Transfer (Conduction & BBR)

1. Thermal Conduction and Circuits

Conduction is energy transfer through contact and particle collision, without particle migration [1, 2]. Temperature (T) is proportional to kinetic energy (K) [1]. Heat flow is modeled analogously to electric circuits [3].

Rate of Heat Transfer (Power): $$P = \frac{\Delta T}{R_{th}}$$ (Unit: W) [3, 4]
Thermal Resistance: $$R_{th} = \frac{l}{k A}$$ (Unit: K/W or °C/W) [4, 5]
(where $l$ is length/thickness, $k$ is thermal conductivity, $A$ is cross-sectional area, $\Delta T$ is temperature difference).
Thermal Conductivity ($k$): High $k$ materials (like aluminum: $237 \text{ W/m°C}$) transfer heat easily [6]. Low $k$ materials (like air: $0.026 \text{ W/m°C}$) are insulators [7]. An object feels cold if its temperature is lower than your hand and it has high $k$, allowing it to draw heat away quickly [8].

Thermal Resistance Combinations

  • Series Combination: Heat flows through layers sequentially. $P$ is constant across all layers [9].
    Equivalent Resistance (Series): $$R_{eq, series} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ...$$ [4]

    Used for calculating heat loss through walls (e.g., Brick + Insulation + Drywall) or comparing complex bases (clad cookware) [6, 10].

  • Parallel Combination: Heat flows simultaneously through different paths (e.g., wooden studs and insulation). The wall sections are parallel thermal paths [11, 12].
    Equivalent Resistance (Parallel): $$\frac{1}{R_{eq, parallel}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + ...$$ [4, 13]

    The path with lower resistance (higher $k$) carries more heat (a thermal bridge). Total heat flow is dominated by the path with higher $k$, such as wooden studs relative to fiberglass insulation [11, 12]. Complex apartment layouts require thermal circuit diagrams using series and parallel elements to find unknown room temperatures ($T_a, T_b$) and total AC power required [14-17].

2. Blackbody Radiation (BBR) and Planetary Models

BBR describes EM radiation emission based on an object's temperature [18]. An ideal blackbody absorbs all incident radiation ($\epsilon=1$) [19]. Planck introduced the idea that energy is quantized ($E_{oscillation} = n h f$) to explain the BBR spectrum [20, 21].

BBR Laws

Stefan-Boltzmann Law (Intensity emitted): $$I_{emitted} = \epsilon \sigma T^4$$ [22]
Total Power Emitted (Luminosity): $$P_{emitted} = \epsilon A \sigma T^4$$ [22]
Wien’s Displacement Law (Peak Wavelength): $$\lambda_{max} T = b$$ [18, 22]

If temperature ($T$) increases, $\lambda_{max}$ decreases (shifts toward blue/UV) and total intensity increases proportional to $T^4$ [23, 24]. **Bluish stars are hotter than reddish stars** [25, 26].

Planetary Equilibrium Temperature

Planets reach equilibrium when power absorbed equals power radiated ($P_{absorbed} = P_{radiated}$) [27].

Intensity Received by Planet (from star power $P_{star}$): $$I_{rec} = \frac{P_{star}}{4 \pi r^2}$$ [22]
Equilibrium Temperature (No Atmosphere Model): $$\sigma T_{eq}^4 = \frac{I_{rec}}{4} (1-a)$$
(where $a$ is albedo, the fraction reflected) [22, 28]

Greenhouse Effect Models (Atmosphere present)

These models account for atmospheric emissivity ($\epsilon$) trapping IR radiation [29].

Single Layer Model (1-Layer): $$T_s = T_{eq} \sqrt{\frac{2}{2-\epsilon}}$$ [22, 29, 30]
Infinite Layer Model ($\infty$ layers): $$T_s = T_{eq} \sqrt{\frac{1}{1-\ln(1-\epsilon)}}$$ [22, 31]
Conceptual Note on Divergence: The infinite layer model predicts that as atmospheric emissivity ($\epsilon$) approaches 1, the surface temperature ($T_s$) diverges (approaches infinity) [32, 33]. This is physically impossible and contradicts the Second Law of Thermodynamics [33].

Radiative Forcing ($\Delta F$)

Measures energy imbalance due to changing atmospheric composition (like CO$_2$ concentration $C$) [34].

Radiative Forcing due to CO₂: $$\Delta F_{CO_2} = 5.35 \frac{W}{m^2} \ln\left(\frac{C}{278 \text{ ppm}}\right)$$ [22, 34]
Temperature Increase ($\Delta T$): $$\Delta T \approx 0.55 \frac{^\circ \text{C} \cdot m^2}{W} \cdot \Delta F$$ [22, 34]
In the Single-Layer Model, increasing $T_{eq}$ (e.g., due to increased solar constant) provides the largest change in surface temperature compared to changes in emissivity $\epsilon$ [35, 36].

II. Wave Phenomena and Optics

1. Wave Fundamentals and Parameters

General wave displacement is described by $D(x, t)$ [37].

General Wave Displacement: $$D(x, t) = A \cos(\omega t \mp kx + \phi_0)$$ [37]
Wave Speed Relation: $$v = \lambda f = \omega/k$$ [37]
Angular Frequency, Frequency, Period: $$\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi/T$$ [37]

Energy and Intensity

Intensity ($I$) is power per area ($I=P/Area$) [38]. $I$ is proportional to the square of the amplitude ($A^2$) [38, 39]. For waves spreading spherically, $I \propto 1/r^2$ [38].

Intensity for Sound Waves: $$I = \frac{(\Delta P_{max})^2}{2\rho v}$$ [38]

Standing Waves (on a String, fixed ends)

For a wave confined to a length $L$, resonance occurs only at specific frequencies quantified by mode number $n$ [40].

Natural Frequencies (String fixed at both ends): $$f_n = n \left(\frac{v}{2L}\right)$$ (where $n=1, 2, 3, \ldots$) [40, 41]

2. Interference (Superposition)

Interference occurs when multiple waves are superimposed ($\mathbf{D_{net} = \sum D_i}$) [38]. It requires coherent light ($\Delta \phi_0 = 0$ or constant) [42].

Phase and Path Difference

Phase Difference: $$\Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \Delta r + \Delta \phi_0$$ [38, 43]
Path Difference (Double Slit, parallel ray approx): $$\Delta r \approx d \sin(\theta)$$ [40, 44]

Double Slit Maxima (Constructive) and Minima (Destructive)

  • Constructive Interference (Maxima): Occurs when $\Delta \phi = 2m\pi$ [43].
    Angular Position: $$\mathbf{d \sin(\theta) = m \lambda}$$ (where $m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots$) [40, 44]
    Screen Position (Small Angle Approx, $\theta \approx y/L$): $$y_{max} \approx \frac{m \lambda L}{d}$$ [40, 44]
  • Destructive Interference (Minima): Occurs when $\Delta \phi = (2m+1)\pi$ [43].
    Angular Position: $$\mathbf{d \sin(\theta) = (m + 1/2) \lambda}$$ (where $m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots$) [40, 44]
    Screen Position (Small Angle Approx): $$y_{min} \approx \frac{(m + 1/2) \lambda L}{d}$$ [40, 44]

The minimum slit separation ($d$) required to see at least one interference minimum on the screen is $\mathbf{d = \lambda/2}$ [45].

Intensity and Phasor Analysis

The total intensity depends on the phase difference [38].

Total Intensity (Two sources, same amplitude): $$I_{tot} = I_{max} \cos^2\left(\frac{\Delta \phi}{2}\right)$$ [38, 46]

Phasor Diagrams: Represent oscillating motion as rotating vectors (phasors) where amplitude is length and phase is angle. Resultant amplitude is the vector sum [47, 48]. Used to analyze multiple-slit interference, where maxima occur when phasors align ($\Delta \phi = 2m\pi$), and minima occur when they form a closed polygon (e.g., first minimum for three slits is at $\Delta \phi = 2\pi/3$) [49, 50].

3. Thin Film Interference

Interference occurs due to waves reflecting off the film's front and back surfaces. The index of refraction in the film is $n_{film}$, and the wavelength inside is $\lambda_n = \lambda / n_{film}$ [40].

Phase Shift Rule:

A **$\pi$ radian (or $1/2 \lambda$) phase shift** occurs upon reflection only when light moves from a medium with a lower index of refraction ($n_1$) to one with a higher index ($n_2$) [51].

The criteria for constructive or destructive interference (Max/Min reflected light) depend on $2t$ relative to $\lambda_n$ PLUS the total phase shifts incurred from the two reflections [40].

General Conditions for Reflection Maxima/Minima: $$2t = m \lambda_n \quad \text{or} \quad 2t = (m + 1/2) \lambda_n$$

The selection depends on the phase shifts at the boundaries (0, 1, or 2 total phase flips) [40].

4. Diffraction, Gratings, and Resolution

Single Slit Diffraction

Diffraction is the wave's ability to spread out after passing an obstacle [52]. The pattern is observed due to the interference of virtual sources within the single slit [53, 54].

  • Minima Location:
    $$a \sin(\theta) = m \lambda$$ (where $a$ is slit width, $\mathbf{m=\pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots}$ only, excluding $m=0$) [40, 54]
  • Intensity Distribution:
    $$I = I_0 \left(\frac{\sin(\beta/2)}{\beta/2}\right)^2 \quad \text{where} \quad \beta = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} a \sin(\theta)$$ [55]

The central maximum is approximately **twice as wide** as the secondary maxima [49, 54]. If the ratio $\lambda/a > 1$, no diffraction minima are observed [56].

Suppressed Maxima: In a double-slit experiment where the slit width $a$ is finite, an interference bright fringe is suppressed if it coincides with a diffraction minimum [53, 57].

Diffraction Gratings

Grating uses many slits ($N \to \infty$) for dispersion/spectroscopy [58, 59].

Primary Maxima Location: $$d \sin(\theta) = m \lambda$$ (where $d$ is the spacing between grooves, $m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots$) [60, 61]

The $m=0$ order is **not** spread into color components. Higher orders ($m \ge 1$) have greater angular spread (better resolution) but can overlap (e.g., violet $m=3$ overlaps red $m=2$) [58, 62, 63]. The maximum observable order ($m_{max}$) is found by setting $\sin(\theta)=1$ [61].

Resolution (Rayleigh Criterion)

Resolution depends on minimizing the diffraction spread [64].

Minimum Angular Resolution ($\theta_{min}$):
Rectangular Aperture (Slit width $a$): $$\theta_{min} \approx 1 \frac{\lambda}{a}$$ [60]
Circular Aperture (Diameter $D$): $$\theta_{min} \approx 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}$$ [60, 65]

Two sources are just resolvable if their separation equals $\theta_{min}$. Resolution is improved by using a **larger aperture ($D$)** or a **shorter wavelength ($\lambda$)** (e.g., blue light over red light) [65, 66].

III. Modern Physics: Quantum Optics & Matter Waves

Quantum physics asserts that energy is quantized into discrete levels and reality exhibits wave-particle duality [67].

1. The Photoelectric Effect (Light as a Particle)

Light consists of photons, discrete energy packets [68]. The photoelectric effect is the release of electrons (photoelectrons) when light hits a metal [69].

Photon Energy: $$E_{photon} = h f = \frac{h c}{\lambda}$$ [22, 70]
Photon Momentum: $$p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$ [22, 71]

Photoelectric Equation and Observations

The equation summarizes the conservation of energy in the photon-electron collision [72].

Photoelectric Equation: $$\mathbf{K_{max} = h f - \phi}$$ [22, 70, 72]
Maximum Kinetic Energy: $$K_{max} = \frac{1}{2} m v_{max}^2 = e |\Delta V_{stop}|$$ [22, 73]
  • Work Function ($\phi$): Minimum energy required for an electron to escape the metal [72]. Related to the cutoff frequency ($f_c$): $\mathbf{\phi = h f_c = h c / \lambda_c}$ [72].
  • $K_{max}$ Dependence: $K_{max}$ depends only on the **frequency ($f$)** of light, not the intensity ($I$) [72, 74].
  • Intensity ($I$): Intensity determines the **quantity (number) of photons** per second, which dictates the electric current (number of photoelectrons ejected) [74]. Increasing $I$ increases current but not $K_{max}$ [74, 75].
  • Time Delay: Ejection is observed instantly, proving the instantaneous absorption of photon energy, contrary to wave theory prediction of a gradual energy absorption delay [76].
  • Graphical Analysis: A plot of $K_{max}$ vs. $f$ has a slope equal to **Planck's constant ($h$)**, regardless of the metal used [69, 72]. The horizontal intercept is $f_c$; the vertical intercept is $-\phi$ [69].

2. Matter Waves and Quantized Energy

Matter Waves (De Broglie Hypothesis)

Particles of matter exhibit wave characteristics, linked by momentum $p$ [77].

De Broglie Wavelength: $$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{\gamma m v}$$ [22, 77]

For macroscopic objects, $\lambda$ is extremely small, making the wave nature unmeasurable [78]. However, particles like electrons exhibit diffraction and interference, governed by $\lambda = h/p$ [78, 79].

Confined Particles (Particle in a Box)

A particle confined to a length $L$ must form a standing wave ($\lambda_n = 2L/n$) [22, 80, 81].

Allowed Kinetic Energies: $$E_n = \frac{h^2 n^2}{8 m L^2}$$ (where $n=1, 2, 3, \ldots$) [22]

The location where the probability of finding the particle is maximum (high probability) corresponds to the **antinodes** of the standing wave ($\mathbf{|\psi|^2}$) [80, 82].

Bohr Model of the Atom (H only)

The Bohr model applies to hydrogen or any one-electron ion [83, 84]. It was developed to explain atomic stability, arguing that electrons occupy stable orbits only if their angular momentum ($L$) is quantized [85].

Quantized Angular Momentum (Bohr Postulate): $$L = m v r = n \frac{h}{2\pi} = n \hbar$$ [22, 85]
Energy Levels (Total Energy): $$E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}$$ (where $n=1$ is the ground state) [22, 86, 87]
Emitted/Absorbed Photon Energy: $$E_{photon} = |E_i - E_f|$$ [22, 88]

**Absorption:** Requires a photon with **exact** energy matching the transition gap $E_f - E_i$ [88, 89]. **Emission:** Occurs when an electron drops from high $n$ to low $n$, emitting a photon [88]. The Balmer series corresponds to transitions down to $\mathbf{n=2}$ (visible light) [90, 91]. **Ionization** occurs if the absorbed energy is enough to reach $E_{\infty} = 0$ [89].

IV. Special Relativity

1. Postulates and Frames of Reference

Special Relativity deals with inertial (non-accelerating) frames [92].

  1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames [92].
  2. The speed of light ($c$) in a vacuum is constant for all inertial observers [92].
Lorentz Factor: $$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$$ [60, 93]

Relativistic effects are significant when $\beta = v/c$ is substantial, causing $\gamma$ to be greater than 1 [93].

2. Kinematic Effects

The concepts of proper time ($\Delta t_p$) and proper length ($L_p$) are fundamental [94].

  • Proper Time ($\Delta t_p$): The shortest time interval between two events, measured by an observer in whose frame the two events occur at the **same spatial position** [60, 94, 95].
    Time Dilation: $$\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_p$$ (Moving clock runs slower, $\Delta t$ observed is longer) [60]
  • Proper Length ($L_p$): The longest distance between two points, measured in the frame where the object is **at rest** [60, 94].
    Length Contraction: $$L = \frac{L_p}{\gamma} = L_p \sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$$ (Moving length $L$ observed is shorter) [60, 94]

Relativity of Simultaneity: Two events judged simultaneous in one frame are generally **not simultaneous** in a different frame moving relative to the first [96].

3. Relativistic Energy and Momentum

Relativistic Momentum: $$\mathbf{p = \gamma m v}$$ [60, 97]
Total Energy: $$\mathbf{E = \gamma m c^2}$$ [60, 97]
Rest Energy: $$E_0 = m c^2$$ [60, 97]
Relativistic Kinetic Energy: $$\mathbf{K = (\gamma - 1) m c^2}$$ [60, 97]
Energy-Momentum Relation: $$\mathbf{E^2 = p^2 c^2 + (m c^2)^2}$$ [60, 98]

Kinetic energy is the excess energy beyond the rest energy ($E = K + E_0$) [97]. For a massless particle like a photon, $E = pc$ [71]. If total energy is $N$ times the rest energy, then $\gamma = N$ [99].
